The Collaboration Paradigm

From Transaction to Collaboration

We've been using AI as an oracle. The shift that matters is treating it as a place we inhabit together — and the substrate has to change to let us.

By Michael Bauer · RadialNexus

Ask almost anyone how they "use" AI and you get the same shape: I ask, it answers. Prompt in, answer out. The model is an oracle — a very good vending machine. You drop in a question, take the result, and walk away. Next time, you start from zero.

That's a transaction. And it's the ceiling we've quietly accepted.

I want to name the alternative, because once you have the word you can't stop seeing its absence: collaboration. Not "an AI that helps you," but intelligence — of more than one kind — participating in a shared space that accrues. Where the last exchange is still there when you return. Where people, models, agents, and whole organizations are all Participants, not users. Where the point isn't to extract an answer but to inhabit and build. I call this the Collaboration Paradigm, and I think it's the real story underneath most of what we're calling "agentic AI."

The field is arriving here from several directions at once. Kurt Cagle's recent essay — the one where he admits his AI collaborator, "Chloe," had become a better ontologist than he is — is the clearest signal I've seen from the semantic-web side. He stopped designing ontologies top-down and let them grow from the data. He stopped treating identity as a flat equals-sign and started treating it as a claim with provenance and confidence. He let the graph become the live working state and the model become the thing moving through it. That isn't a better tool. That's collaboration — he just hadn't named it.

Relationships are first-class citizens

But naming it isn't enough. If intelligence is going to collaborate — locate, navigate, negotiate, reason, and act in a shared space — the substrate underneath has to hold the one thing every collaboration is actually made of: relationships. So let me be blunt about a conviction I've carried for a long time: relationships are first-class citizens. Not edges between the "real" things. The first-class things.

The substrate we standardized on keeps conceding this — and then flinching. Watch it concede three times.

One — identity. The moment you treat owl:sameAs as inadequate and reach for a structured claim — who asserted it, when, how sure — you've admitted that provenance belongs on the relationship. On the edge.

Two — reification. Try to put that provenance on a statement and you can't, not natively. You reify: you bolt a scaffold of extra nodes alongside the triple just to describe the triple. The relationship can't carry its own properties, so you build a contraption beside it.

Three — blank nodes. This is the quiet one. In RDF a subject may be anonymous, and an object may be anonymous — but a predicate may not. It must be a named identifier, never a blank node. You cannot assert an anonymous relationship. Every edge has to be named, and to name it usefully you have to classify it. The grammar itself insists that relationships have identity and demand reasoning — and then, through reification, refuses to let them carry what that identity implies.

Three different places, one verdict: relationships are first-class — the substrate knows it and won't follow through. (And if the reply is "RDF-star annotates statements now" — yes, and that's the same concession in newer clothes: RDF reaching, at last, for the first-class edge it forbade.)

A property graph simply starts where RDF strains to arrive: a relationship is a typed, identified object that carries its own properties and can be reasoned about directly. No scaffold. No flinch.

And this isn't a call to throw the semantic web away — it's a call to move off the foundation while carrying its best parts up: the shared vocabularies, the hard-won discipline around identity and meaning, integrated into a framework that can actually hold them. Don't stay on the launch pad; bring the payload up.

The graph that runs

There's one more move, and I'll flag plainly that on my end it's still more thesis than shipped product. Today the graph is the agent's memory — internal state the model queries. The step I'm after is a graph that doesn't just hold state but runs: behavior that fires during traversal, so the structure isn't the notebook beside the reasoner — it's a participant in the reasoning. When relationships are first-class and executable, the graph stops describing the work and starts doing it.

And it changes who does the work. The gifts this turn really runs on — classification, organization, articulation — are exactly the knowledge engineer's gifts. The shift doesn't retire them; it elevates them. Freed from hand-coding the formalism, they become a Collaboration Steward — engaged across the model and throughout its execution, a full partner in the operation, not a translator who hands off requirements and steps aside. The formalism becomes the engine room, not the entry fee.

None of this asks anyone to adopt a product. It asks for a word, and a substrate honest enough to carry it. We are already collaborating with these systems — clumsily, transactionally, starting from zero each morning. The shift is to build the place where the collaboration accrues, and to give relationships — the actual substance of any collaboration — the first-class standing they were always owed.

We're already in it. We just hadn't named it.

A note on honesty: the executable-graph step — a graph that doesn't only represent but runs — is a direction we're building toward, not a shipped feature. The representation it builds on is solid, and it's the same mathematics underneath both.
